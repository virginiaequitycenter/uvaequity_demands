ANALYSIS

We collected 50 years of documents written by Students, Faculty, Staff, and Working Groups, all addressing racial inequity at the University of Virginia. Our team of qualitative researchers read each document, broke them down into specific demands, and grouped those demands into categories to understand concrete domains for institutional change.

These categories range from specific actions like Minority Student Recruitment to Living Wage for Staff Members to more genaral comments around categories such as School Climate . For a closer look at the categories and their meanings, take a look at our Methodology page.

As we prepared this project, we had to consistently remind ourselves that repeated asks, longer documents, and louder voices are not always indicative of the most urgent or important actions. One pithy demand does not take up as much space as a ten point plan. But it can bear as much power. Not to mention people are often pragmatic. Sometimes demands represent what people feel will be accomplishable without giving voice to their deepest hopes and dreams.

As you engage with our work, think of this as a conversation. It is not an exclusive or exhaustive list of the work to be done nor a comprehensive poll of public opinion. Instead, it is a record of history to honor those who envisioned what the University could be long before it started to accept that vision for itself.

Who Speaks?

Above, we looked at when specific topics enter the conversation. But we also need to consider who said them. Which groups speak on which issues? Who speaks together? So we grouped the documents by six author categories: (1) Faculty/Staff, (2) University Offices & Commissions, (3) Black Students at UVA, (4) APIDA Students at UVA, (5) Latinx Students at UVA, & (6) Groups of students who do not specificy demographic affiliation – these groups include interracial coalitions. We made these classifications using the authors' self-identification in the documents themselves. We then summed the number of recommendations made in each category by each group over time.

Tabulating these recommendations by authors, we were struck by the number of times the University spoke about itself in the form of working groups and Presedential commissions. Excluding recommendations recorded in the 2017 Index of Diversity Recommendations (a University document summarizing prior student documents), we recorded 330 discrete actions demanded of the University by itself. You can explore those items more through our Interactive Timeline.

  • For groups that do not have many written records, what have they spoken about?
  • In what ways does our focus here on number of actions demanded deemphasize other important factors?

A Few of Our Takeaways

Thought (& Action) Leaders: Did you see that Black students at the University have spoken as an identity group on every key topic here but one (Reparations)? Next to the University, they are the most prolific demographic in this set. In particular, Black students have led the converstation on UVA's relationship with the Charlottesville Community. Take a look at Community Labor or Partnership Current Initiatives and Working Groups & Living Wage.

Money Matters: The University speaks frequently in this set, but not on Abolition/ Defunding, a category that includes Divestment/Responsible Corporate Finance & Removal of Statues. That said, faculty groups have broached the topic before.

Precision, Not Breadth: There is quite a bit of literature here. As we noted at the start, sometimes forcing comparisons based on written quantity of discrete demands deeemphasizes the important contribution of pithy documents or activism outside of the written word. For example, visually looking at the graphic above, you might not have spent much time examining APIDA and Latinx contributions. And yet, the #We Are Not Invisible and Our University To Shape documents sparked so much conversation among other groups represented in this analysis. They made precise and actionable asks. Use the Visual Library to look for other instances of poignant but powerful speech.

Conversational Consensus: All groups have spoken on the need for Minority Faculty / Staff Recruitment and Retention and some restructuring of Research or Coursework toward a more antiracist and inclusive bent.


What Are They Saying?

After knowing who spoke on which topics, we wanted to get a sense for what types of words were used in each category. Specifically, we wanted to know what key terms and actions were distinctive or most unique to each category. To do that, we used a metric called keyness.

Keyness compares the distribution of words in one set to the distribution of words in another. You can think of it as a comparative measure of occurence. For example, if we were comparing fruit in Florida to fruit in other states, Pineapples would probably have a high keyness in Florida. If you are interested, you can read more about keyness here.

We calculated keyness and chose the top ten most distinctive words in each category. We display those distinctive words in the circular bar chart below. Bars are ordered from highest to lowest keyness. You can hover over each bar to see the key word as well as the number of times that word was used in the demand category.

Questions to ask as you look include:

  • Are there any words that you want a broader context for? (Hint: Remember them, that's coming next!)
  • How do key words give you a sense for the category as a whole?
  • Are there any words that surprise you?
  • Find the adjectives. What kinds of people and systems are being discussed?
  • Are there any words that go together?

A Few of Our Takeaways

Access & Progression Did you see the key term Graduate in the Minority Student Recruitment section? While demands in this category certainly do address UVA's undergraduate enrollment of minority students, there is a particular focus on providing access to higher education to BIPOC beyond the undergraduate level.

Responsible Contracting: Both Contracted and Attorney are keywords in the Living Wage category alongside Living, Worker, Benefit & Employee. These terms are in reference to the Attorney General of Virginia's 2006 opinion that there is "No authority granted to University of Virginia’s Board of Visitors to require that minimum or living wage be paid by private contractors and vendors to their employees. Virginia Public Procurement Act does not authorize requirement of living wage in public procurement process." [citation] This is an opinion many would like the university to challenge to ensure that all those who work on Grounds are justly compsensated.

Supportive Networks & Resources: The category School Climate comments on how to make the University of Virginia more culturally welcoming to BIPOC. And yet, we know that following recommendations in all the other categories would also contribute to that goal. So how is this category different? Looking at the key words, we see topics such as increasing Mentorship, considering student's relationship with Alcohol, routing services to those who most Need them regardless of demographic factors, and requesting that the administration make firm Statements in support of BIPOC, particularly during specific local, state, and national events. These are all concrete actions directly targeted and creating a more inclusive Climate at UVA.

Adding Context

Did you find some key words above that you wanted to explore further? Or maybe keywords just do not give you as clear a picture into the content as you would like. It is true, they can sometimes be vague. When we saw the word Need in School Climate, for example, we had to get more context before we could understand what it was talking about too. Keywords are a 10,000 foot view into the richness of these documents.

So below, we provide a keyword in context (KWIC) search platform for you to take a bit deeper of a dive. If you are like us, maybe you will get lost for an hour exploring all of the references to the Latinx and APIDA communities. Or maybe you just want to see how people have historically addressed the Board of Visitors. That said, if you want even more context and direct access to the texts, it is definitley time to head over to our Interactive Timeline.

To search, type in a word or a word stem with a * at the end (e.g. support* will give you support, supportive, supporting, etc...) to see demands that include that word.
